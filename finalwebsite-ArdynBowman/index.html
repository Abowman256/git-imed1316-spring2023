What Is Fine Art?

If you are going by the European-centric definition, the idea of fine art is art that is created with no real function. Paintings, mosaics, and sculptures can be some examples of art created with only artistic or aesthetic ambitions in mind. Art for the sake of art.

We have had this idea of art for art’s sake for a very long time. In some definitions music and performance are also included in the idea of “fine art”, which shows how embedded and essential fine art is to the fabric of our lives. Yet, this idea of fine art really reached a fever pitch during the Italian Renaissance. A very important moment in art history, it was a time when a lot of creative theories were really starting to come into focus and when “fine art” really got defined. This is the main reason fine art usually refers to works from this period and onward.

It is obvious in hindsight why the idea of fine art would have gained traction, recognition, and respect in 15th-century Italy. All of Europe was coming off the heels of the black death during this time. An unimaginable pandemic that wiped out up to half of the European population, which, rightfully so, felt near apocalyptic to those living through it. These circumstances created a change in not only how the public viewed fine art but also the fine art that was being created. All this death made it so a lot of the art being created was centered on life and expressing emotions. This aesthetic artwork, which maybe had no practical function, served the purpose of an outlet for expressing the value of the human experience.

To this day fine art serves a similar important emotional function for many people. Most people probably have a piece of art that has affected them in some way, even if it is as simple as being their favorite.
